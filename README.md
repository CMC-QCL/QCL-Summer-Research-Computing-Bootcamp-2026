# Welcome to our Summer Tech Bootcamp!
Different questions, different methods. Many of you will spend at least part of this summer working with data, code, or computational tools. We are excited to invite you to the QCL Summer Bootcamp designed to help you do that work better, faster, and more efficient.

The theme tying everything together is "Reproducible Research".

## What is reproducible research?
Reproducible research means that someone else (or your future self, six months from now) can take your data, your code, and your documentation and arrive at the same results you did. It's the difference between "I ran some analyses and got this chart" and "here is exactly how this chart was made, and anyone can verify it."

## Why should you care, even as an undergraduate?
- Your work becomes credible. Reviewers, advisors, and future collaborators can trust your findings because they can check them.
- You save yourself from future pain. Most researchers have lost hours (or weeks) trying to remember what they did three months ago. Reproducible practices are really just kindness to your future self.
- It's becoming a baseline expectation. Top journals, funders, and employers increasingly require reproducible workflows across the sciences, social sciences, and humanities alike.
- It makes collaboration possible. Your faculty advisor, your lab partner, and the student who picks up this project next summer can all build on your work.

## What we'll cover
The bootcamp introduces a toolkit you can mix and match based on your project:
GitHub — version control and collaboration
Python — basic coding, virtual environment setup, and AI-assisted (vibe) coding
R — data analysis, scripting, and data visualization
GIS — spatial data analysis and mapping
HPC for Research — remote computing tools (batch script, Bash, SSH, etc.) for projects that outgrow your laptop

### A note to students across the disciplinary spectrum:
Whether your work is quantitative, qualitative, archival, experimental, or theoretical, these tools have something to offer. The same habits that keep a research workflow reproducible also apply to text analysis, coded datasets, and structured arguments. If you're newer to computing, this is a chance to build a foundation; if you already code, this is a chance to sharpen your habits.

No prior experience is required. Sessions are modular, so you can take what fits your project.
